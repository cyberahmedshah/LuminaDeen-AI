Section 01

The History and Significance of Nisab

The concept of Nisab is central to the Islamic obligation of Zakat. Understanding its historical origins provides deeper insight into the wisdom behind this threshold and its relevance to modern Muslims.

📜 Origins in the Prophetic Era

The term "Nisab" (نِصَاب) comes from the Arabic root meaning "origin" or "starting point." In Islamic jurisprudence, it refers to the minimum amount of wealth one must possess before Zakat becomes obligatory.

During the lifetime of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, the Nisab was established through divine revelation and prophetic tradition. The two primary standards were set:

🥇

Gold Standard

20 Dinars — approximately 87.48 grams of pure gold.

🥈

Silver Standard

200 Dirhams — approximately 612.36 grams of pure silver.

📜

"No Zakat is due on less than five Uqiyyah of silver, and no Zakat is due on less than five Dhawdi (a camel load) of grain."

— Sahih al-Bukhari

⚖️ The Wisdom Behind Two Standards

Many people question why there are two different Nisab standards. The historical context provides clarity:

In 7th century Arabia, both gold and silver served as currency. The ratio between them was approximately 1:10 — one gold Dinar was worth about 10 silver Dirhams. Both standards represented roughly equivalent purchasing power.

Today, this ratio has drastically changed. Silver is now significantly cheaper relative to gold. This has led to scholarly discussions about which standard to apply.

🕌 Evolution Through Islamic Civilization

The Rightly Guided Caliphs (632–661 CE)

Abu Bakr رضي الله عنه famously declared war against those who refused to pay Zakat, demonstrating its non-negotiable status. Umar رضي الله عنه established the first formal Zakat administration, creating a treasury (Bayt al-Mal) for collection and distribution.

The Umayyad and Abbasid Periods

As the Islamic empire expanded, scholars codified Zakat laws. The four major schools of thought (Madhabs) developed detailed rulings on Nisab, largely agreeing on the core thresholds while differing on some applications.

The Ottoman Era

The Ottoman Empire institutionalized Zakat collection as part of state administration. They maintained the gold and silver standards but also adapted to local currencies across their vast territories.

🌐 The Modern Challenge

Today's Muslims face a unique challenge: the massive divergence between gold and silver values. Using the silver standard results in a much lower Nisab threshold, making Zakat obligatory on more people. Using the gold standard creates a higher threshold.

🥈

Silver Standard Argument

  • Benefits more poor and needy recipients
  • Follows the principle of caution (Ihtiyat)
  • Aligns with the spirit of wealth purification
🥇

Gold Standard Argument

  • More stable value over time
  • Better represents the original purchasing power
  • Less burdensome on modest earners

🎓 Scholarly Positions

Most contemporary scholars and Islamic finance institutions recommend:

1️⃣

Conservative Approach

Use the silver standard to maximize benefit for Zakat recipients.

2️⃣

Proportional Approach

Consider the type of wealth — gold wealth against the gold Nisab, silver wealth against the silver Nisab.

3️⃣

Local Guidance

Follow the prevailing scholarly opinion in your own community.

The fundamental principle remains unchanged from the time of the Prophet ﷺ: Zakat purifies wealth and supports those in need. The Nisab threshold ensures this obligation falls only on those with sufficient means.

Section 02

Who Must Pay Zakat?

Zakat is one of the five pillars of Islam. It is a mandatory act of worship and a financial obligation for eligible Muslims. Below are the conditions that make Zakat obligatory on a person.

☪️

Muslim

Zakat is only obligatory on Muslims. Non-Muslims are not required to pay it, though they may give charity (Sadaqah) voluntarily.

🧑

Adult (Baaligh)

Zakat is obligatory only upon adults who have reached the age of puberty (Baaligh). Children's wealth is generally not subject to Zakat according to the majority of scholars.

🧠

Sane (Aaqil)

The person must be of sound mind. Scholars differ on whether Zakat is obligatory on the wealth of those with mental incapacity; the guardian may pay on their behalf as a precaution.

💰

Nisab Threshold Met

The person must own wealth equal to or exceeding the Nisab threshold — the minimum amount of wealth above which Zakat becomes obligatory.

📅

Hawl Completed

The wealth must have been owned for one complete lunar year (Hawl — approximately 354 days). Agricultural produce is an exception; Ushr is due at each harvest regardless of Hawl.

🔓

Free Ownership

The person must have full, unencumbered ownership of the wealth. Debt-financed assets or wealth held in trust for others may be excluded.

📜

"Take from their wealth a charity by which you purify them and cause them to increase."

— Surah At-Tawbah (9:103)

Section 03

Nisab — The Minimum Threshold

Nisab is the minimum amount of wealth a Muslim must possess before Zakat becomes obligatory. There are two Nisab standards, both established from the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ's teachings.

🥇

Gold Nisab

87.48 grams
  • Based on the weight of pure gold
  • Currently worth more in monetary value
  • Results in a higher threshold (fewer people liable)
  • Used when a person owns primarily gold
Most contemporary scholars recommend this for gold holders.
🥈

Silver Nisab

612.36 grams
  • Based on the weight of pure silver
  • Lower monetary threshold due to silver prices
  • More inclusive — more people fall under obligation
  • Many scholars recommend for cash/savings
Preferred by scholars for wider charitable coverage of the poor.

🤔 Which Nisab Should I Use?

Scholars differ, but a common recommendation is: if you own gold, use the gold Nisab for your gold; for other wealth (cash, savings, business), use the silver Nisab. This broader approach ensures more Zakat reaches those in need. Always consult a trusted scholar for your specific situation.

Section 04

What Assets Are Zakatable?

Not all wealth is subject to Zakat. Islamic scholarship distinguishes between assets that are zakatable (productive/liquid wealth) and those that are not (personal use assets).

✅ Zakatable Assets

💵
Cash & Bank Balances

All cash at home, current accounts, savings accounts. Rate: 2.5%

🥇
Gold & Silver

All gold and silver jewellery, coins, and bars — whether worn or stored. Rate: 2.5% on market value

📈
Stocks & Investments

Shares held as long-term investments. Rate: 2.5% on market value (or underlying zakatable assets per share)

🏢
Business Assets

Cash held in business, trade inventory/stock at market value. Rate: 2.5%

🌾
Agricultural Produce

Crops and harvest at time of harvest. Rate: 10% (natural irrigation), 5% (artificial), 7.5% (mixed). No Hawl required.

🏠
Investment Property

Property held for resale: 2.5% on full market value. Rental property: 2.5% on accumulated net rental income.

❌ Non-Zakatable Assets

🏠
Primary Residence

The home you live in is not zakatable, regardless of value.

🚗
Personal Vehicle

Your car, motorcycle, or transport used for personal use is exempt.

🛋️
Household Furniture & Appliances

Items used for daily household needs are not zakatable.

👗
Personal Clothing

Clothes worn for personal use — including gold/silver jewellery for personal use (per some schools; others include it).

🏭
Business Equipment

Machinery, tools, and equipment used in the production process (not for sale) are exempt.

Section 05

Zakat on Agriculture (Ushr)

Zakat on agricultural produce is known as Ushr. Unlike other forms of Zakat, Ushr is due at each harvest and does not require a full lunar year (Hawl) to pass. The rate depends on the method of irrigation.

🌧️

Natural Irrigation

10%

Rain, river, spring, or any naturally flowing water. Since no additional cost is incurred by the farmer, the higher rate applies.

Example: If crop value = ₨100,000 → Ushr = ₨10,000
⚙️+🌧️

Mixed Irrigation

7.5%

When both natural and artificial methods are used equally throughout the growing season, the average rate of 7.5% applies.

Example: If crop value = ₨100,000 → Ushr = ₨7,500
⚙️

Artificial Irrigation

5%

Pump, well, purchased water, or any irrigation requiring ongoing cost and effort by the farmer. The reduced rate accounts for the expenses incurred.

Example: If crop value = ₨100,000 → Ushr = ₨5,000

📌 Key Points about Ushr

  • No Hawl (lunar year) is required — Ushr is due at every harvest.
  • Applies to all types of agricultural produce: grains, fruits, vegetables.
  • There is a minimum quantity threshold (Nisab for crops = 653 kg of grain equivalent in most opinions).
  • Production costs (seeds, labour, fertiliser) are not deducted before calculating Ushr according to the majority opinion.
  • Ushr is calculated on the gross value of the crop at harvest time.
📜

"On that which is watered by the sky and springs, or is flood-irrigated, the Ushr (one-tenth) is due; and on that which is irrigated by the well, half the Ushr."

— Sahih al-Bukhari (1483)

Section 06

Zakat on Property

Property (real estate) is one of the areas where Islamic scholars have different scholarly opinions. The Zakat treatment depends entirely on the intent and use of the property.

🏗️

Property for Resale

2.5%

If you purchased property with the intention of reselling it (trade/investment), it is treated like trade inventory and Zakat is due at 2.5% of its current market value after one lunar year.

Example: A plot bought at ₨5,000,000 now valued at ₨7,000,000 → Zakat = ₨7,000,000 × 2.5% = ₨175,000
🏘️

Rental Property

2.5% on income

If the property is rented out for income, most scholars hold that Zakat is not due on the property itself, but the accumulated rental income (after expenses) is zakatable at 2.5% if it remains above Nisab at the end of the Hawl.

Example: Annual rental income = ₨240,000. Expenses = ₨40,000. Net remaining = ₨200,000 → Zakat = ₨5,000
🏠

Personal Use Property

Exempt

Your primary residence or any property used personally (holiday home for personal use, family home) is completely exempt from Zakat. This is agreed upon across all major schools of jurisprudence.

Note: Even if your home is worth millions, no Zakat is due on it as long as it is your personal residence.

⚠️ Important Scholarly Note

There is scholarly disagreement on property Zakat, particularly for residential properties held as investments. Some scholars say 2.5% applies to the full value; others restrict Zakat only to income generated. We recommend always consulting a qualified Islamic scholar (Mufti) for property matters, as the ruling can vary based on your madhab (school of jurisprudence) and specific circumstances.

📜

"There is no Zakat on a house a man lives in, on servants he uses, or on a horse he rides."

— Reported by Ibn Hazm, Al-Muhalla

Section 07

The 8 Recipients of Zakat

Allah ﷻ has specified exactly who may receive Zakat in the Quran. These 8 categories are fixed and cannot be changed.

📜

"Zakat is only for the poor, the needy, those employed to collect it, those whose hearts are to be reconciled, for freeing captives, for those in debt, for the cause of Allah, and for the stranded traveller."

— Surah At-Tawbah (9:60)

01
🤲

Al-Fuqara

The poor — those with little or no income, unable to meet basic needs.

02
🍞

Al-Masakin

The needy — those who have some income but it is insufficient to cover essential needs.

03
⚖️

Al-Amileen

Zakat collectors and administrators — those employed to collect and distribute Zakat.

04
❤️

Al-Mu'allafatu Qulubuhum

Those whose hearts are to be reconciled to Islam — new Muslims or those who may be brought closer to the faith.

05
🔓

Fir-Riqab

Freeing of captives and slaves — historically for manumission; contemporary applications include freeing people from oppression.

06
💳

Al-Gharimin

Those in debt — people who have borrowed for legitimate needs and are unable to repay.

07
🌍

Fi Sabilillah

In the cause of Allah — supporting efforts that serve Islam and the Muslim community, including education and charity work.

08
🧳

Ibnus Sabil

The stranded traveller — a wayfarer who is far from home and without sufficient funds, even if wealthy at home.
